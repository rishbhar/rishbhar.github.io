Constraints provide us with the scope of our design. They bound us in limits, but that is where we start to push it and explore creative ideas. Uber ratings redesign project was about embracing constraints and understanding their importance in the design process. For the project, we knew who our users were but we had to decide what problem we would be solving for them. We had to come up with a DESIGN CORE and a MANTRA! We tried to make sure that our CORE guided us to our design, and that our MANTRA resonated through it.
Design a more compelling rating system

We were asked to design a more compelling rating system for Uber. It could have been a complete overhaul of their system, or a small variation. We also had to keep in mind that the design was going to be a part of a larger system (the Uber app).


Why is it a 5 star rating system and...

We had to understand what really the problem was, and to begin, we asked questions. We began with understanding the current Uber system. We brainstormed about Uber's rating system, and came up with questions like:

Why do riders rate drivers and vice versa?
Why only numbers for ratings? Why not something else?
Why is there only one parameter for rating the whole experience?
Is the system biased towards riders?
How does the rating system affect a short term and a long term driver?
Is there something for expectionally good/bad behaviour by a driver?
How motivated are the riders to rate their ride? and How often do they rate their ride?
Do riders understand how the system affects drivers? Do they need to?

and many more... While we were exploring, we also started sketching.

Choosing to design for quick and constructive feedback

We continued our secondary research to get more information on the subject. We found out that there has been a lot of agitation among the drivers for Uber's rating system. We interviewed two drivers in Bloomington and got to know about their opinion on the rating system. All this research told us a lot about the drivers and how they were trying to cope up with the consequences of the rating system. But we knew little about the big population of riders, so to get an idea we built a survey form Uber Passengers Survey , with a few questions and shared it with people on social media, twitter etc.

From research and interviews:
1) If a driver's average rating is anything below 4.6, he is in the danger zone.
2) Experienced drivers knew that even a single rating below 4.5 from a rider can affect their employment with Uber.
3) Most of the riders had no idea that anything below a 5 could drag down a driver's rating into that danger zone.

These insights were surprising! It made us realize how things, that we take for granted, could be of such consquences. And through our insights, came our design core

Design Process - Looking through the lenses

We were introduced to the concept of LENSES in the project prompt. "Lenses, taken in order, provide a kind of brainstorming activity. Each lens builds on the output of the previous one. This framework is designed to iteratively develop a tangible and actionable output of methodologies and best practices.” (from the prompt)

Lenses which helped us the most were.


UNDERSTANDING THE PROBLEM

We began with the lens for reframing the problem into an answerable question. We started coming up with questions about everything that we didn't understand in the prompt. We compiled all the questions that we found compelling and stepped into the research phase.

SKECTHING & BRAINSTORMING

We learned that sketches sparked conversation. We came up with more ideas and questions when we started sketching out our ideas. We sketched as many ideas we could by following the YES, AND approach.



Trying out icons for feedback
A thumbs up/down for a quick first level feedback





NEGATING & STRENGTHENING

At this stage, we had a design that we could critique among ourselves. We started to think out the situations/reasons our design might not work! and obviously there were some.

1) Users might not want to rate a driver as their favorite.
2) A rider might think that he cannot clearly communicate the ride experience by just giving a thumbs up or a thumbs down.
3) Icons/Visuals work well, but do they for everyone? Specially when you when want to communicate attitude, friendliness etc.

We changed the design by not including icons. We gave preference to getting a more constructive feedback than saving riders a few seconds. That was our core. (a little trade-offs-in-design lesson for us)


USABILITY TESTING

We went out to the local mall to test our design, and also talked to students who had used Uber to test our design. During our tests, we got a comment by one of the users which made a really good addition to our design. We added an option to 'opt out of a driver', using which riders can avoid having a ride again with a driver they had a very bad experience with.






What I learned

1) I learned how to effectively use storyboarding to present a design. Also, my story should be able help me as a designer to empathize with the user in the story.
2) I learned to embrace constraints and identify trade offs.
3) That we should design, fail fast and make it better by iterating.
