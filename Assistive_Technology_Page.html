Interaction Design

This project aimed to target a problem for a specific group of people, the Unexotic Underclass. Problems of these groups usually go unnoticed. The groups themselves receive scant attention from entrepreneurs and investors. Also, they don't have access to the very best which is available in the market or cannot afford it. So, for us this project was a pathway to understanding how we design a solution to a specific problem for one of these groups. We were asked to decide on a group and the problem we wanted to design for, which made the whole process more challenging and intriguing!
Medical prescriptions for the functional illiterates

We can end up in a very difficult situation if we are not able to follow our medical prescriptions correctly. In some cases, not following the prescription correctly could even lead to hospitalization. Or it can have some dangerous side effects! For functional illiterates, (those that speak English, but struggle with reading and writing) this was one of the big problems that they faced in their daily lives. The inability just doesn't affect them but could also comprosise the health of their family members.


Current medical landscape, and the solutions available

The information on the packages, bottles and prescriptions can be overwhelming. Besides the medicine intake, these packages and medicine bottles have a lot of information on side effects and warnings too. With all this information, we were getting to know a little about what could cause problems for a functional illiterate. We also started looking up already available solutions for the problem.

The textual overload on bottles, packages etc, can be overwhelming for functional illiterates


We found out that most of the mobile solutions were more focussed towards being reminders apps. They were text heavy, with minimum visual aids. However, there were other good solutions like the PillPack from IDEO which gave us a lot to think about.



Currently available applications which are mostly reminder apps


We also met with Bethany Teryy, Coordinator for the Literacy program, VITAL at the Monroe County Public Library in Bloomington. From her we got to know that they used a lot many iPad and mobile applications for teaching the functional illiterates.

We were also able to squash a few our assumptions using user journey maps. To gather information for the journey map, we met with a few pharmacists.


Meeting with Bethany Terry, VITAL
A patient's journey map



Insights that audio and visual aids would work better if they are simple

1) These people were embarrased about their problem.
2) It affected them more for accute illnesses.
3) Audio and visual aids are very helpful for learning new things.
4) They could understand hand drawn simple icons more easily.
5) They wanted to do this independently without much help from others.



Clearly establising what is our design, and what is not

We are designing for
  people who can comprehend spoken english
  have acute illness
  adults who are also responsible for others
  information on the medication packaging
  people with a smartphone

We are not designing for
  second language speakers
  teaching how to read
  replacing the doctor or pharmacist
  monitoring medication intake
  identifying bottles outside their prescription




Core - Making medical prescriptions and labels explicit to functional illiterate adults
Cindy is spending time with her son, Tyler, in the living room




She receives a notification on her cell phone




The application reminds her of her medicine dosage for 1:00 pm. She clicks on the notification




She reads her name and clicks on "OPEN" to open her prescription for 1:00 pm




She can see her medicine for 1:00 pm. She wants to confirm which medicine is AMOXIL, so she clicks on it.




She can see the AMOXIL bottle now, which displays its dosage information. She can also hit the speaker button to hear out bottle information.




As she hears out the information, she compares the bottle with the image in the application




As the application speaks out the bottle information, it highlights the same on the bottle image so as to help Cindy follow the information better.




Cindy checks out the warnings for the medicine. Warnings are being shown through simple hand drawn icons.

Design Process - Reflecting back on methods that stood out
EXPANSION & CONTRACTION

As this project didn't has it all laid down about the user group and their problem, we had to explore different user groups and their problems.



Expansion for different user groups and their problems


Trying to narrow it down by exploring particulars problems


While doing this exercise of expansion and contraction, we continued doing extensive research. It helped us decide on our user group and the problem we wanted to solve for them.


DECIDER PROTOCOLS

The problem landscape grew with expansion. There were multiple problems for each user group. So using the decider protocols for this project really helped us narrow down. At times, it also enabled us to express our opinions when we thought differently.


ROUND ROBIN SKETCHING

We did round robin sketching, and did a lot of it! It helped us build of off each other's ideas, and unify them all. It helped us understand each other perspective of what we thought solutions could be like, and it was a great way to learn from each other!



A time constraint activity where we build off of each other's skctches

NEGATING & STRENGTHENING

I asked the team that I wanted to iterate on our design using the themes of good design. So out of the 7 themes of good design, I used 4 that could be used to iterate.

1. Good design is human-centered, not machine-centered.

Having affordances to take the burden off the user



2. Good design employs the system as a transparent medium.

More cues for the user to understand context



3. Good design creates computer imaginative interactions.
4. Good design provides for ease of learning.

Computer imagination exploits the strengths of the medium



What I learned

1) Clearly establishing what the design is, and what it is not stabalizes decision making.
2) Using journey maps for an unknown user group and their problem helps squash assumptions.
